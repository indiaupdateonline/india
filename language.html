The Sanskrit Tradition

Sanskrit, meaning "refined speech," is the classical language of ancient India and the liturgical language of Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism. With literary records dating back to 1500 BCE, Sanskrit boasts one of the richest literary traditions in human history.

The Sanskrit corpus includes sacred texts like the Vedas and Upanishads, epic narratives like the Mahabharata and Ramayana, classical poetry and drama by Kalidasa and others, as well as extensive works on philosophy, science, mathematics, and more.

Sanskrit Literary History

Major periods and developments in Sanskrit literature

1500–500 BCE

Vedic Period

Composition of the four Vedas (Rig, Sama, Yajur, Atharva), Brahmanas, and early Upanishads. Sacred hymns and ritual texts forming the foundation of Hindu thought.

Explore Vedic Texts
500 BCE–200 CE

Epic Period

Composition of the great epics Mahabharata and Ramayana, along with early Buddhist and Jain texts in Sanskrit. Development of classical Sanskrit grammar by Panini.

Explore Epic Texts
200–1200 CE

Classical Period

Golden age of Sanskrit literature with works by Kalidasa, Bhasa, Bhavabhuti, and others. Flourishing of poetry (kavya), drama (nataka), and scholarly literature.

Explore Classical Texts
1200–1800 CE

Medieval Period

Continued composition of philosophical and commentarial literature. Sanskrit remains language of scholarship despite rise of regional languages.
